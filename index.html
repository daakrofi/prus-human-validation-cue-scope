Instructions

You are about to be presented with a series of 500 Steam discussion posts published before the named game was released. Each item contains the post title and the captured post text used in the study. Your task is to decide whether each post contains PRUS.

What is PRUS?

Pre-Release User Speculation (PRUS) is the user-to-user expression and exchange of low-commitment conjectural propositions about an unreleased product, produced under conditions, and in service of making sense, of incomplete product information.

Simply put:

PRUS occurs when users put forward an uncertain idea about an unreleased game: what it may contain, how it may perform, what may be required to access it, or what using it may be like. The idea can be a claim, guess, prediction, explanation, interpretation, theory, or unresolved alternative. It does not have to be a firm prediction or use future tense.

How to Code Each Post:

Read the title and the full displayed post as one connected contribution to the discussion. Select PRUS when at least one sentence or connected passage does both of the following: it puts forward a claim, explanation, prediction, interpretation, or unresolved alternative about the game or product experience; and it presents that idea as possible, tentative, inferred, hypothetical, unverified, assumed, guessed, or otherwise not fully certain. One sentence may contain both parts, or nearby statements may supply them together. The entire post does not have to be speculative. For a PRUS post, select every product topic domain that applies to the relevant passage or passages.

The two-part PRUS check

Both parts must be present somewhere in the title or connected post text.

1

Identify the idea being put forward.

Look for a possible claim, explanation, prediction, interpretation, theory, or unresolved alternative about the game or the experience surrounding it.

2

Check that the user reduces commitment to that idea.

Common signs include may, might, could, probably, possibly, seems, I think, I suspect, it looks like, not sure, conditionals, and alternatives the user leaves unresolved. Equivalent informal wording also counts.

An uncertainty word without an uncertain idea is not enough. A future event is not automatically PRUS. A direct assertion, opinion, complaint, or expression of excitement is not PRUS unless the post also advances an uncertain idea about the product or product experience.

Questions, requests, hopes, and suggestions

These forms can express PRUS when they put a possible product state, explanation, or outcome into discussion. Their grammatical form alone does not make them PRUS.

PRUS

A question that directly raises a possible product state.

“Could the locked area be another map in the full game?”

PRUS

A question that is phrased as a request but advances an explanation.

“Why show three empty class slots unless more classes are planned?”

PRUS

A hope that also introduces an uncertain product possibility.

“I hope the empty slot means another class is coming at launch.”

PRUS

A suggestion that includes an uncertain consequence.

“They should improve the servers because they may struggle with the launch crowd.”

Not PRUS

A question seeking only a currently available fact or fix.

“My controller isn’t working. Which setting fixes it?”

Not PRUS

A pure request or preference without an uncertain product idea.

“Please add split-screen.”

Not PRUS

A question that asks only for a currently knowable release detail.

“Which edition includes early access?”

Code the possible idea, not the question mark, request, hope, or suggestion by itself. A question is PRUS when it proposes or explores what may be true of the game. It is Not PRUS when it only seeks a fact, instruction, or immediate troubleshooting answer and does not raise a wider uncertain possibility about the product.

Demos, betas, and Early Access

Current experiences with a pre-release build can supply evidence for an uncertain interpretation of the wider product. A bare report of what happened is not enough.

PRUS

A current experience used to infer a possible release-state problem.

“The demo keeps crashing, so the launch build may have the same issue.”

PRUS

A current experience that raises a possibility about the wider game.

“The combat feels limited in the demo. Is this all there will be?”

PRUS

A concern about how a pre-release problem may develop.

“The beta performance is rough. I’m not sure they can fix it before launch.”

Not PRUS

A direct report of the current build without an uncertain interpretation.

“The demo crashed twice today.”

Not PRUS

A resolved technical problem.

“The demo crashed because my graphics driver was outdated. Updating it fixed the problem.”

A pre-release problem counts when the post uses it to raise or imply an unresolved possibility about the game or product experience. A current-state report that stops at what happened, or explains and resolves the problem, does not count.

Do not code these as PRUS on their own

  • Direct factual claims, even when they concern the future.
  • Ordinary excitement, dislike, praise, criticism, or sarcasm stated with full commitment.
  • Negation by itself, such as “the demo is not good.”
  • Advice, obligation, permission, or ability, such as “players should restart” or “the game can run at 60 FPS.”
  • Technical questions that seek only a fact, instruction, or immediate fix.
  • Resolved technical explanations and discussion unrelated to the product or product experience.

Content

Speculation about story, mechanics, characters, modes, maps, features, monetization, or gameplay experience.

Example: "The demo probably hints at a bigger crafting system."

Performance

Speculation about technical quality, optimization, FPS, bugs, crashes, servers, or launch stability.

Example: "It might run badly on older GPUs at launch."

Requirements / Access

Speculation about platform access, hardware specs, storage, regions, editions, release timing, or play conditions.

Example: "The full version may need more RAM than the demo."
